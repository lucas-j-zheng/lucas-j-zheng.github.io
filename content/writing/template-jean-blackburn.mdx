*Written by Lucas Zheng*
*Jean Blackburn, **Template**, 2002. Wood and paint. RISD Museum, Ways of Looking Exhibition.*
*[View in the RISD collection](https://risdmuseum.org/art-design/collection/template-20133)*

---

The rocking chair has been gutted. Its spindles snapped off like ribs. Its arm has been amputated. Its seat has been cored out. And it is, somehow, still standing and still rocking. A few feet away sits its smaller counterpart, created out of the entrails of the first. This is Jean Blackburn's *Template*, and it's the most violent thing I've ever seen done to a piece of furniture that still works as furniture.

I didn't see any of that the first time I looked. I saw two beat-up chairs: one mutilated, one small and out of place. I almost walked past. The smaller one looked like a stripped-down piece of junk, its paint chipped away in patches. It took another look to realize the paint hadn't been chipped off. It had been put on. And it had come from the chair beside it.

A huge bite, taken as casually as one out of an apple, removes nearly half the seat. Two surgical swings of a baseball bat at the same angle dislocate the spindles into cartoonish broken teeth. A few broken spindles still hang from the crest rail above, dangling like stalactites that never finished forming. The left arm wasn't cut. It was torn out of its socket, leaving an exposed wood hole and a few chips of blue paint scattered around it like collateral. Wherever anything is removed, raw wood shows through the blue paint, like skin pulled back to expose the bone underneath. Through it all the chair stands. The rocking chair rocks.

A few feet away, the smaller chair sits perfectly still. It is everything the big one isn't. It's complete, intact, child-sized but real. Its coloring is almost perfectly symmetrical. Blue patches ring the outside of the seat in an almost oval. The crest rail is bare wood. The outer stiles framing it are blue, matching each other on either side. The armrests each have a speckle of paint on the outer side. The two chairs share a color budget, and blue lost from one tends to show up on the other. The small chair's skin is the big chair's bones. Nothing was destroyed. Everything was moved.

Look at how the pieces came off. The seat bitten, the spindles swung at, the arm excised. No two acts of removal come the same way. Each body part is taken differently. The art isn't the sculpture. The art is the harvest itself — strategically transplanting parts from one chair into the other. The small chair got the symmetry, the completeness, the pattern. The big chair got just enough to keep standing. Function and form usually live in the same object. Blackburn redistributes them. Add the two chairs together and you get exactly one chair's worth of material. The big one kept the function and gave up the form. The small one took both, diminished.

It feels easiest to call this what it looks like at first glance. Mother and child. Parent and offspring. Big and small. But that's cheating. It's too emotional, too sentimental, the classic cop-out reading based on nothing but appearance. The title provides the clue for what this actually is. *Template.* Not a parent. A blueprint. The thing other things are made from. Most objects have a template somewhere, like a mold in a factory, that we will never see. The chair you're sitting in right now came from one. So did the desk it's pushed under. Blackburn shows us the template doing double duty: the source the small chair was built from, and the blueprint it was built to.

*Template* works. It works because Blackburn combined calculated brutality, craft, intention, and the logic of a chair that still rocks. Without the small chair, the big one is just a source of construction materials. A pile of broken wood. The small chair is what makes the destruction legible. It's the answer to the question the wreckage is asking.

Look at what's gone. The spindles, decorative. One arm, comfort. Half the seat, non-structural. Look at what stayed. The rockers. The base of the legs. The stretchers. The crest rail, somehow still held up. Every piece Blackburn removed was a piece the chair didn't need to function. Every piece she kept was load-bearing or part of the rocking infrastructure. The wreckage isn't random. It's surgical. Blackburn could have stripped it of everything that makes a chair but took it apart for proof. The chair still rocks because she made sure it could. That's the part worth pausing on. Holding the big chair together while taking enough out of it to build a complete second chair is a severely limiting constraint. Either you wreck the source or you compromise the product. Blackburn refused to do either.

This is why the piece looks like junk at first. Blackburn refused to dress it up. She didn't smooth the breaks or sand the wounds or hide what it took. She left the math on the table. Every cut is visible. Every transplant is visible, decisive, and very much needed. Most artwork just shows you the result. Blackburn pries artwork apart and shows you both halves of it — the art and the work. The result and the process.

Descent is inescapable. The structure carries on. The big chair is still rocking, missing the piece it gave away. The small chair is still sitting, wearing what it took. And both are intact in their own way.
